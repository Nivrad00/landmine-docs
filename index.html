Fri 1-29-21

First meeting with client and HDFS team

Darvin's notes:

  • VN sort of thing
  • progress through a full school day?
  • some other scenarios (others in powerpoint): bullying -- dirty looks; being called on in class
  • art - tova volunteered to draw, jamie said her sister has experience in graphic design, and marisa also said she has undergrad connections she could rope in. may use stock art for now
  • cartoon or realistic? cartoon seems to be more effective based on research
  • VR compatibility - our project will be more of a prototype to get feedback from kids, but if we can choose art that is more amenable to VR (eg art that's somewhere in the middle) that's great
  • what degree of freedom -- should they be able to move around? maybe somewhere in the middle between linear and full freedom.
  • is there a way to win? marisa doesn't want it to be too much about "winning" or it might make kids feel worse.
  • so how do you tell kids whether they've made good responses or not? it would be a bit boring if we just told them straight away "wrong." maye if you make too many wrong responses you have to "go to school again the next day."
  • we discussed the possibility of a hidden/visible score system and mostly settled on having a "feelings thermometer."
  • positive feedback would be great, like positive interactions if you do the right thing
  • how much do we want to show negative feedback? we want them to learn from that, but we don't want to show too much.
  • maybe you can use deep breathes/mindfulness/tactile playing with things to improve the mood on the thermometer
  • voice acting is a possibility -- marisa has some budget, especially since she will be getting voice acting eventually for the final VR intervention (where kids play VR in a room with a clinician)
  • it's probably not possible to add full CBT stuff into this game -- preparing, going through a single experience, and then debriefing

Second client/team meeting (Tova's notes + Marisa's comments)

1) We want the school environment to feel safe, or at least safe enough for the intervention to work without causing too many outliers in the data collection 2) We want the school environment to be somewhat customized, so that students can choose their class agenda/schedule that day. 3) The user should be able to choose gender (and gender nonconforming identities) as well as race. These choices may determine the experiences that the user has throughout the game/school day *Going off that note, we want to ensure that our language is carefully chosen, as to not cause unnecessary harm/trauma to the user 4) The game's environment should look like North Carolina and/or that region of the US, and the US in general at the very least 5) There should be a range of student/adult and student/peer relationships: good, bad, complicated, etc. Perhaps a crush, a bully, a group of "cool kids," a supportive school counselor, a nonchalant teacher, etc. 6) We need a justification of use. Dr. M is going to provide us with that language, so that we can direct our sights appropriately. 7) This is a fairly large age range 13-18 years of age. A day in middle school vs. a day in high school may not look very different in a game, but perhaps the language used, social interactions, and bullying might. 8) With regards to the user's chosen identity at the beginning of game, we need to ensure that those choices are important to the trajectory of the game in some fashion. What makes identity/character variation so important with regards to how the game itself will be programmed? 9) What is the most triggering aspect of this simulation? The fact that the user has biologically-driven depression and is entering the school environment? The school environment itself? The interaction of several different personality types/social circumstances faced? After Dr. M left, we spoke about: 10) The logo. Jamie's sister has been so kind as to her offer her services in this respect. What do we want the logo to look like? More importantly, what should the title of this game be? (Hmm, great question. Open to ideas, prefer not to have the word suicide in it though.) 11) Darvin brought up a great point about motivating the user throughout the game--what will this look like? Jamie and Morgan had fantastic ideas about perhaps framing this intervention as a "first day of school" or "first day back after hospitalization" in order to give the user more encouragement to push past their comfort zone. (Framing it as first day return is perfect.) To piggyback off that idea, after hospitalization, many kids at school may be nosey regarding the user's absence. Facing questions like "Where've you been?" or "Are you crazy or something?" may be good examples of what interactions would look like during the game. (Yep, exactly. I am hoping Jamie, Morgan, and Noelle can start drafting some of the scripts for each of the scenarios I sent in the ppt last week.) 12) With regards to data collection, should every question reflect a metric, or can there be some filler language/scenarios to make the game seem and feel more organic? (No, please add filler. Min. data collection required is once per each scenario.) 13) Morgan came up with a GREAT idea of assigning each user a "comfort contact." This person will function as the "help" button in any other game, but this person will be someone who regularly interacts with the user as they face their day of challenges. This comfort person can be selected either at the beginning of game or during school day. (Cool!) 14) Wayne brought up a good point: we don't want users to feel like they've made "bad" or "wrong" decisions. Instead of following up a "wrong" decision with more "wrong" decisions (as typical of other games), how about we introduce a coping mechanism every time a person chooses a "wrong" decision, so that we give the user an opportunity to boost their mood thermometer? In essence, a "wrong" decision will just lead to an opportunity to recover. ( Love this idea.) 15) Darvin wanted to ask Dr. M if this intervention was going to extend to afterschool activities, or stay within the confines of the school building?
